The Screen · Five

Roger Penrose & Stuart Hameroff

Understanding is something no computation can do.

The last few pages have circled one suspicion: that mind is not just clever machinery. This page makes that suspicion into an actual argument — a careful, technical one, built by a mathematician who won a share of the Nobel Prize in physics, and then given a home inside the brain by a doctor who puts people to sleep for a living. Roger Penrose starts from a famous piece of mathematics and claims it proves that a human mind can do something no computer ever could. Stuart Hameroff takes that claim and asks: then where, in the wet tissue of a real brain, could such a thing be happening? Their answer reaches all the way down to a structure smaller than a cell and out to the deepest laws of physics. It is the hardest material in this book — so we will go slowest of all here, one small step at a time. A stranger should be able to follow every step.

Start with the thing on your desk

Think about a pocket calculator, or the computer you are reading this on.

Everything it does, it does by following rules. Type 2 + 2, and it does not understand two, or addition, or anything at all. It runs a fixed recipe — take this symbol, take that symbol, apply this stored step, then the next — and out comes 4. The recipe is called an algorithm: a list of exact instructions that could be followed by something with no understanding whatsoever, just the ability to shuffle symbols by the rules. That is what a computer is. A machine for following algorithms, blindingly fast, and blind.

Now here is the assumption almost all of modern science makes about you. It says: your brain is also a machine following rules — vastly more complicated ones, but rules all the same. Neurons firing, signals passing, all of it a kind of computation. On this view, your mind is just what a very large, very fast algorithm feels like from the inside. Thought is computation. There is nothing else going on.

Penrose says: that cannot be the whole story. And to show why, he reaches for one of the strangest discoveries ever made about rules themselves.

A truth you can see but cannot prove

Picture a box.

Inside the box is a system of rules — a complete, exact method for proving mathematical statements true. Feed it a claim about numbers; if the claim is provable, the box will grind through its rules and stamp it PROVED. This is what mathematicians dreamed of building: one trustworthy machine that could, in principle, prove every mathematical truth. A perfect rule-follower.

In 1931 a young logician named Kurt Gödel proved that no such box can exist — and the way he proved it is the hinge of this whole page, so hold on and take it slowly.

Gödel showed how to build, for any such box of rules, one special sentence. Call it G. The sentence G is carefully rigged to say, about itself:

“This sentence cannot be proved by the rules in the box.”

Now think about what the box can do with G. Suppose the box proves G. Then G would be provable — but G says it is not provable, so the box would have proved something false. A box that proves falsehoods is broken; we assumed ours was sound. So a sound box cannot prove G. But wait — that is exactly what G says! G says “I cannot be proved,” and we have just worked out that, indeed, the box cannot prove it. So G is true.

Sit with the shape of that. The box cannot prove G. Yet you — standing outside the box, looking at what it says and what it can do — can see that G is true. Not by following the box’s rules; the rules are precisely what cannot reach G. You saw it by understanding what G meant and reasoning about the box from the outside.

That is the crack Penrose drives his whole argument into.

So understanding is not rule-following

Here is the move. Whatever method you just used to see that G is true, it was not the box’s set of rules — because those rules provably cannot get to G. You reached a truth that no algorithm inside that system could reach.

Now, someone will object: “Fine, but maybe your mind is just a bigger box, with more rules.” Penrose’s reply is the heart of it. Whatever box you claim the mind is, Gödel’s trick builds a new G for that bigger box too — and again the human mathematician, understanding what it means, can step outside and see it is true. You can always climb out. No fixed box of rules ever contains your ability to understand. That is not true of any computer, which is forever trapped inside whatever rules it was given.

Penrose states the conclusion plainly, and it is worth reading in his exact words:

“Human mathematicians are not using a knowably sound algorithm in order to ascertain mathematical truth.” — Roger Penrose, Shadows of the Mind (1994), p. 76 [verbatim]

Unpack that one careful sentence. He is not saying humans never make mistakes, or that we don’t often think in rule-like ways. He is saying that the specific act of understanding a truth — really grasping why G must be true — is doing something no rule-following machine can do. Understanding is non-computable. There is a step in genuine human thought that is not, and cannot be, the running of an algorithm.

If that is right, it is enormous. It means the mind is not a computer — not a faster one, not a bigger one, not any one at all. Something else is happening. And a physicist who believes that is now obliged to ask a very hard question: where, in the physical world, is there anything that isn’t a computation? Because if the brain is made of ordinary physics, and ordinary physics is computable, then Penrose has painted himself into a corner. His escape is the boldest part of the story.

Where in all of nature could a non-computer hide?

Penrose went looking through physics for a place where nature does something not reducible to following a rule. He found one candidate — and it lives in the strangest room in all of science: quantum mechanics.

Go gently. In the everyday world, a thing is in one definite state — a coin is heads or tails. But the tiniest things, like a single particle, are described before you look at them as being in a blur of possibilities at once — a bit like a coin still spinning in the air, not yet landed, genuinely both. Physicists call this blur a superposition. Then you measure it — you “look” — and the blur suddenly snaps to one definite answer. Heads. The spinning stops. This snapping-to-one-answer is called collapse, and here is the scandal at the center of physics: nobody agrees on what causes it, or what “looking” even means. The equations describe the smooth spinning beautifully. They say almost nothing about why, or when, it lands.

Penrose has his own proposal for what does it, and it is characteristically bold: gravity. Every mass bends space around it, ever so slightly. When a tiny object is smeared across two places at once, its gravity is smeared across two places too — and Penrose argues nature will not tolerate that blur of space itself for long. The heavier the object, the more unbearable the blur, the faster it must resolve. A single electron could hang spun-up for millions of years; a speck you could see under a microscope would collapse almost instantly. He calls this gravity-driven collapse objective reduction — objective because it happens on its own, out in the world, with no observer needed to “look.” (This is the same instinct that made him say the real puzzle isn’t quantizing gravity but “gravitizing” the quantum — letting gravity be what decides.)

And crucially — this is why he went hunting here in the first place — Penrose argues that how the blur resolves into one specific answer is not a computation. It is not the running of any algorithm hidden in the particle. It is a genuine, one-off, non-computable event in the fabric of reality. He believes he has found, in objective reduction, the one place in known physics where nature does something a computer cannot: it chooses, without following a rule.

So now he has both halves. A mind that does something non-computable. And a physics with exactly one non-computable event in it. The temptation to join them is obvious. But there is a gap a mile wide: quantum blurs are famously delicate, snuffed out the instant they touch anything warm and wet — and a brain is nothing if not warm and wet. Who could possibly connect a fragile quantum event to a living neuron? That is where the second author walks in.

The turn: down inside the neuron

Stuart Hameroff spent his career as an anesthesiologist. His whole job is to switch consciousness off and on with gases — and to wonder, every day, what exactly am I switching? He had long suspected the answer was not in the big obvious wiring of the brain, the neurons passing signals like a telephone network, but somewhere smaller and stranger.

Inside every neuron, holding it in shape like tent-poles, are tiny hollow tubes called microtubules — lattices built of a protein, far smaller than the cell itself. Hameroff proposed that these tubes are not just scaffolding. They are, he suggested, the actual machinery of consciousness: small enough, structured enough, and (he argues) shielded enough that a quantum blur could briefly survive inside them — protected from the warm chaos long enough to matter.

Put Hameroff’s tubes together with Penrose’s gravity-collapse and you get the theory this page is named for: Orch OR — orchestrated objective reduction. The idea, in plain words: inside the microtubules, quantum blurs build up, “orchestrated” (tuned, arranged, made meaningful) by the living activity of the neuron around them. Each blur grows until it hits Penrose’s gravity threshold — and collapses. That collapse, the non-computable event, is proposed to be a flicker of consciousness. Not consciousness caused by the brain afterward, like heat off an engine — the collapse itself, they claim, is a moment of raw experience. String millions of these flickers together, orchestrated across the brain, and you have the ongoing stream of a conscious mind.

Notice what a strange and daring bridge this is. It ties the most intimate thing you have — the felt fact of being aware right now — to the deepest and coldest layer of physics, the structure of space and time. That is not an accident of the theory; it is the whole point. If consciousness rides on gravity-driven collapse, then the thing that makes you you is woven into the basic fabric of the universe. Mind is not a late accident of biology. It is stitched into the floor of reality.

The nugget

A computer only ever follows rules — yet a human mind can look at a rule-bound system, step outside it, and simply see a truth the rules can never prove. So understanding is not computation; something in thought reaches past every algorithm. Penrose finds the only non-computable event in known physics — the gravity-driven collapse of a quantum blur — and Hameroff hides it inside tiny tubes in your neurons. On their telling, each collapse is a spark of experience, and your mind is the universe’s deepest physics, orchestrated into feeling.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If — and here, more than anywhere, the honest work is to cut one claim cleanly into two, because the two halves are on completely different footing, and it is a mistake (a common one) to accept or reject them together.

The first half — the Gödel argument — is real philosophy, and genuinely unsettled. It is not a crank’s trick; it has been argued over by serious logicians for decades. But it is contested, and you should know the strongest objection. Critics answer: yes, you can step outside the box and see G is true — but in doing so you have simply moved to a larger box of rules (one that includes “the original box is sound”). You haven’t escaped computation; you’ve just switched to a bigger algorithm. Penrose has replies to this, and the critics have replies to the replies — the logician Solomon Feferman and others pressed exactly this point, and it has never been cleanly settled either way. The honest status: the Gödel argument is a live, respectable, unproven philosophical case that understanding is non-computable — not a theorem that settles it. It shifts where the burden lies; it does not win the match.

The second half — the microtubule biology — is on much weaker ground, and here most experts say plainly that it is wrong. The killer objection is called decoherence. Quantum blurs are exquisitely fragile; the warmer and busier the surroundings, the faster they collapse into ordinary randomness — and the inside of a living brain, at body temperature, drenched in jostling molecules, is about the most hostile place imaginable for a delicate quantum state. The physicist Max Tegmark ran the numbers and argued the blurs in a microtubule would die in a fraction of a fraction of the time Orch OR needs — far too fast to do any orchestrating. Hameroff and Penrose dispute this and point to newer findings of “quantum vibrations” in microtubules as encouragement. But it is fair and important to say: the great majority of neuroscientists and physicists do not accept the microtubule theory, and regard the quantum-brain claim as unproven at best. The two authors are honest that the whole thing lives or dies as testable science — which is a virtue — but as of now, it has not passed.

So cut them apart. You can find the Gödel argument genuinely provocative — a real reason to doubt that mind is only computation — while treating the specific microtubule machinery as a fragile bet that the evidence does not currently support. Taking the philosophy seriously does not oblige you to buy the biology.

And now the fault line that sets up the next page. The thinker who follows, Karl Friston, comes at the mind from the exact opposite direction — and the collision is head-on. Penrose’s entire case is that the mind does something no computation can do. Friston’s entire case is that the brain, the self, the whole feeling of being someone, is a computation — a specific one: a process of inference, a machine forever guessing what is out there and correcting its guesses. Where Penrose says “step outside the algorithm,” Friston says “the self is nothing but the algorithm running.” One of them locates the essence of mind in the non-computable; the other builds mind out of computation and finds no remainder. That is not a small disagreement to be split down the middle. It is two of the sharpest living minds pointing at the same organ and saying opposite things about what it fundamentally is. Turn the page and meet the one who says: it’s inference, all the way down.

“We proposed in the mid 1990’s that consciousness depends on biologically ‘orchestrated’ coherent quantum processes in collections of microtubules within brain neurons… This orchestrated OR activity (‘Orch OR’) is taken to result in moments of conscious awareness and/or choice… We conclude that consciousness plays an intrinsic role in the universe.” — Stuart Hameroff & Roger Penrose, “Consciousness in the universe: A review of the ‘Orch OR’ theory,” Physics of Life Reviews 11 (2014): 39–78, Abstract.
