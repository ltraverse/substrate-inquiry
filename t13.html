The Screen · Three

Philip Goff

Matter feels, all the way down.

The last two thinkers pulled the solid world out from under you — first showing your senses are a headset, then that mind might come before matter. Philip Goff is a living philosopher who comes at the same mystery from the opposite end: he takes the hard, ordinary, scientific world completely seriously — atoms, electrons, the equations of physics — and asks one quiet question about them that almost no one thinks to ask. The answer, followed honestly, leads somewhere that sounds absurd at first and then very hard to shake. This page walks it one small step at a time, starting from something a child could describe. Go slowly; the strangeness is earned, not assumed.

Start with the simplest question about anything

Pick up any object near you — a cup, a coin, a phone. Now try to say what it is.

Watch what actually comes out of your mouth. “It’s heavy.” “It’s cold.” “It’s hard.” “It falls when I drop it.” “It rolls.” Every single thing you can say is really a description of what the object does — how it behaves, how it pushes on your hand, how it moves when you push back. Heavy means it pulls down. Hard means it resists when you press. Cold means it draws heat out of my skin.

Notice what you never once said: what the thing is, in itself, when nothing is pushing on it and no one is looking. You described a whole list of its doings — and somehow never touched the being underneath them.

Hold onto that gap. It is small, it is easy to miss, and it is the crack the whole page opens through.

Physics widens the crack, it does not close it

You might think: fine, that’s everyday talk — but science tells us what things really are. Science looks inside.

Look at what it actually finds. Ask a physicist what an electron is, and you get its mass, its charge, its spin. But press on any one of those words and it dissolves into more doing. Mass? That’s how strongly the thing pulls on other things and resists being pushed. Charge? That’s how strongly it shoves or tugs other charged things. Spin? Again — how it behaves in a magnetic field. Every term physics offers turns out to be one more description of what the electron does to other things. Goff puts it in five plain words: “Physics tells us what matter does, but not what it is.”

This is not a flaw in physics. It is what physics is for. Physics is a fantastically precise map of behaviour — it lets us predict, build, launch rockets, split atoms. But a map of what everything does is still, from top to bottom, silent about what the stuff is that’s doing it. Imagine a book that describes a game only by its rules — what each piece may do, where it may move — and never tells you what the pieces are carved from. You could play forever and never learn whether they’re wood or ivory or light. Physics is that rulebook. It is complete as a rulebook, and it never once mentions the material.

So the crack from the cup is not closed by going deeper. It goes all the way down. Right at the bottom of the physical world there sits a question physics is not even built to ask: these particles have a nature — they are something — so what are they?

The turn: the one thing you already know from the inside

Here is Goff’s move, and it is quieter than it sounds.

There is exactly one piece of the universe whose inside you know directly — not by watching what it does, but by being it. Your own experience. You don’t have to measure your toothache to know it hurts; you don’t infer the redness of a sunset from its behaviour; you live it, from within. Every other thing in the world you know only from the outside, by its doings. This one thing — consciousness — you know from the inside, as what it is.

So Goff asks the simplest possible question. We have a world full of matter whose doing we understand perfectly and whose being is a total blank. And we have one and only one clue to what “being, from the inside” is even like — the fact of experience. What if the blank and the clue are the same thing? What if experience is not something special that switches on inside a few soft grey brains, but is simply what matter is, underneath everything it does?

That is panpsychism. In Goff’s words: “mass, spin, charge, etc, are, in their intrinsic nature, forms of consciousness.” Not that an electron thinks, or worries, or has a tiny face. But that at the bottom of physical reality there is some unimaginably simple flicker of experience — that a fundamental particle is, as he puts it, one of the “fundamental physical entities — perhaps electrons and quarks — possessing extremely rudimentary forms of consciousness.” The felt inside is not built up out of dead parts at the top. It was there, in the simplest form, at the very bottom. All the way down.

Why physics was never going to find this on its own

If that sounds like it should have shown up in a physics experiment by now — here is the piece Goff adds, and it is the reason his book is called Galileo’s Error.

Go back four hundred years, to the birth of modern science. Galileo wanted to describe the world in the clean, exact language of mathematics. But experience — the redness of red, the sound of a sound, the sting of pain — cannot be written as a number. It doesn’t fit the equations. So Galileo made a deliberate decision, a brilliant and fateful one: he set experience outside of science. He drew a line, put all the qualities-you-feel on the far side of it, and kept for physics only what could be measured and counted. That single choice is what let physics become the powerful, precise thing it is.

But look at what follows. If, at the very start, you defined your science so that consciousness is the one thing it doesn’t handle — then of course, centuries later, your science can’t explain consciousness. It was built not to. Asking physics to find experience is like sorting your mail into “letters” and “not-letters,” throwing out the second pile, and then being amazed you can’t find any packages. Goff’s point is not that physics is wrong. It is that physics has a hole in it by design, exactly where consciousness would go — and panpsychism is the proposal to fill that hole with the one thing we already know from the inside.

There is a last step Goff takes, to fix a real problem. If every particle has its own separate speck of experience, how do billions of them ever add up to the single, unified you looking at this page? Little bits of experience don’t obviously glue into a big one. So Goff leans toward the reverse: maybe the fundamental conscious thing is not the tiny particle but the whole universe — one vast field of experience — and you, and I, and every electron are regions of that single consciousness, the way one ocean has many currents. This is cosmopsychism: not countless little minds adding up, but one cosmic consciousness, breaking into the many. (Remember that word one — it will matter again soon.)

The nugget

Physics only ever tells you what matter does — never what it is. There is one thing in all the universe whose inside you know directly, by being it: experience. So the simplest answer to what matter is, underneath all its doing, is the thing you already are — consciousness, present in some faint form all the way down to the electron, and physics missed it only because it was built, from the start, to leave it out.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, not As-Proven — and the honest work here is to see that Goff’s argument has a strong, sober half and a daring half, and to keep them apart.

The strong half is the gap itself: that physics describes only the behaviour of matter and is genuinely, structurally silent about its intrinsic nature — what the stuff is in itself. This isn’t mysticism or word-play; it’s a point serious philosophers of science have granted for a century, and you can feel its force yourself with the cup in your hand. Physics gives us the rulebook and never the material. That much stands up.

The leap is what fills the gap. That the intrinsic nature is consciousness is not something the argument proves — it’s the simplest candidate Goff can find, because it’s the only intrinsic nature we have any direct acquaintance with. But “simplest and only clue we have” is a reason to take it seriously, not a proof that it’s so. The blank could be filled by some nature we’ve never conceived, neither mind nor mere matter. Goff argues panpsychism is the most elegant, least wasteful option on the table — and elegance is a real reason, but it is not evidence. Held As-If, it’s a live and disciplined bet; sold As-Proven, it would be exactly the kind of beautiful pattern that flatters us into skipping the check.

And now the disagreement that earns the next page. The thinker who follows, Giulio Tononi, is a neuroscientist with a precise theory of consciousness — and he and Goff clash on one enormous question: how widespread is experience? Goff says it is everywhere — intrinsic to matter itself, so a lone electron carries its faint flicker. Tononi says no: consciousness exists only where information is deeply integrated — woven together into a unified whole — and he even has a measure for it, a quantity he calls Φ (“phi”). Where Φ is high, like in a living brain, there is rich experience. Where it’s low or zero — a lone electron, a bucket of sand, even a digital computer built the wrong way — there is little or none. So Goff’s universe is saturated with mind down to the last particle; Tononi’s has mind only where the wiring integrates, and vast stretches of matter feel nothing at all. Both take consciousness with total seriousness. They simply disagree, fundamentally, on how far it reaches. Turn the page and meet the one who says: not everywhere — only where it all comes together.
