The Screen · Two

Bernardo Kastrup

There is only one mind; we are the ways it splits apart.

On the last page Donald Hoffman took the world apart and found, behind the screen, not dead matter but experience — and he said that experience was many: a countless crowd of separate little minds. Bernardo Kastrup agrees with the first part completely and refuses the second. He is a computer scientist and philosopher who spent years arguing, carefully and stubbornly, for one plain idea that sounds impossible when you first hear it: there is only one mind in all of reality, and you are one of the ways it has split itself apart. This page walks you there slowly, starting from something that happens to you every single night. Take it gently; the strangeness is earned one small step at a time.

Start with your own dreams

Tonight you will fall asleep, and your mind will build a world.

Not remember one — build one. In the dream there is a place, with its own ground and sky. There are other people in it, who say things you didn’t plan and surprise you. There is a “you” walking around inside it, looking out through dream-eyes. And here is the part worth stopping on: while you are dreaming, you do not know any of it is you. The stranger who frightens you in the dream feels like someone else entirely. The room feels like a real room, out there, solid, not yours. You are fooled by your own mind, from the inside, every night.

Then you wake up, and the trick is obvious. There was no room and no stranger. There was only one mind — yours — and it had quietly divided itself into a world, and a self inside the world, and other selves who seemed separate and were not. One thing, wearing the mask of many.

Hold that, because it is the whole shape of what Kastrup is going to claim about everything. A single mind can, all by itself, throw up the appearance of a whole world and many separate people — and can do it so well that the people inside don’t know they’re one.

A stranger fact than dreams: minds that wall themselves off

Dreams end when you wake. But there is something that does not end, and it is not a theory — it is in the medical books.

Some people, usually after great pain early in life, develop what doctors call dissociation: the mind walls off a part of itself so completely that it becomes, for all practical purposes, a separate person. One body, one brain — but two or more selves living in it, each with its own name, its own memories, its own voice. And they do not share. One “alter” (that is the word for one of these split-off selves) can be awake while another has no memory of what just happened, the way you’d have no memory of someone else’s afternoon.

It goes deeper than talk. This is the part that stops people. In documented cases, one alter has been measurably blind — the eyes work, the brain’s seeing-parts work, but that self cannot see — while another alter, in the very same body, sees perfectly fine. The wall between the selves is real enough to switch sight on and off. So this is not pretend and not metaphor: a single mind can divide itself into genuinely separate centers of experience, each sealed off from the others, each certain it is a whole and separate person.

Now you have two facts sitting side by side. One mind can build a world and populate it with seeming-others (your dreams). And one mind can split into truly separate, sealed-off selves that don’t know they’re one (dissociation). Kastrup is about to put them together and aim the result at the entire universe.

The turn — one mind, and we are its alters

Here is the leap, and it is a big one, so take it in slow.

Kastrup says: start by throwing out the usual picture. The usual picture is that reality is fundamentally matter — dead stuff, particles, fields — and that somehow, in a few wet lumps of it called brains, this dead stuff learned to feel. That last step is the hard problem, the one this whole book circles: how could anything made of unfeeling matter ever light up into an inside, an experience, a someone home? Nobody has ever shown how. It may be the wrong question.

So Kastrup turns the whole thing over. What if there is no dead matter to begin with? What if the one and only fundamental thing is mind — experience itself — and everything that exists is a movement, a pattern, inside it? He gives that one universal mind a name: mind-at-large. Not a god, not a person with plans. Just the single field of raw experiencing that is, on this view, the base of reality — the one dreamer, if you like, that everything is happening inside of.

And you — you are an alter of it. A dissociated pocket of the one mind, walled off the way one of those clinical selves is walled off, so completely that you feel like a wholly separate person adrift in a foreign world. Every living creature is another such alter. We are not separate minds that somehow appeared in a dead cosmos. We are the one mind, dissociated into countless sealed-off selves, each certain it is alone.

What, then, is the physical world?

But wait — if it’s all mind, why does the world look so solidly not like mind? Why is there a hard table, a heavy stone, a brain you can slice open and find no thoughts inside, only grey tissue? This is the objection everyone raises, and Kastrup’s answer to it is the cleverest move he makes.

Think again about the alters in one body. When one alter looks at another — or when a doctor looks at the patient — what do they see? They cannot see the other self’s inner life directly. They see it from the outside: a face, a voice, behavior, a body, brain scans. The rich inner experience is real and full on the inside, but from across the dissociative wall it appears as something physical — a body, an organ, a pattern on a screen. The inside, seen from outside the boundary, looks like matter.

Now scale that up. Kastrup’s claim is that the entire physical world is what the rest of mind-at-large looks like, seen from inside our little dissociation. Matter is not a different substance from mind. Matter is what mind looks like from across a boundary — the outside view of an inside you can’t directly enter. Your brain is not what produces your experience; your brain is what your experience looks like to me, from out here, on the far side of the wall between us. And the whole vast physical universe — stars, rocks, the cold dead-seeming reaches of it — is the outward appearance of the one mind’s own inner life, viewed from within one of its pockets.

He puts the living case sharply. Every living thing is an alter — so a living body, seen from outside, should be exactly what a dissociation-in-progress looks like. In his own words: “metabolizing organisms are simply what universal-level dissociative processes look like.” A living creature is not a machine that mysteriously grew a soul. It is the visible outside of one of mind-at-large’s split-off selves — you are looking at the far side of someone’s inner life, and calling it a body.

And now watch the hard problem: it is gone. There is no longer any puzzle about how dead matter makes experience, because there was never any dead matter — only experience, and matter was just its outside appearance all along. The gap everyone was trying to bridge turns out to have nothing on the far side of it. That is Kastrup’s whole prize: not a solution to the hard problem, but a claim that the problem was an illusion created by starting from the wrong end.

The nugget

There is only one mind. You are not a separate spark of awareness stranded in a dead universe — you are that one mind, walled off from itself into a seeming-separate self, the way a single dreamer becomes a whole cast of strangers, the way one clinical patient becomes several sealed-off people. And matter is not the opposite of mind: matter is simply what a mind looks like from the outside, across the wall. Your brain is the outside of your inner life; the whole physical world is the outside of the one mind’s inner life, seen from within.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, not As-Proven — and here the honest work is to cut one clean line through the middle of the page, because part of it is checkable fact and part of it is a vast bet, and it matters enormously which is which.

What is genuinely checkable is the machinery Kastrup borrows: dissociation is real. That a single mind can split into separate, sealed-off centers of experience — each with its own memories, each blind to the others, in one documented case even switching eyesight on and off between selves — is not mysticism. It is in the clinical literature; you could go read the case studies. So the raw capacity he needs — one mind appearing as many, convincingly, from the inside — is established. That is a real and surprising fact about minds, and it does real work: it shows the “one mind, many selves” idea is at least possible, not incoherent.

What is the leap — and it is an enormous one — is everything that comes after: that the whole universe is a single such mind, dissociating; that all matter is mind-viewed-from-outside. Nothing in the clinical facts forces this. You can grant every word about dissociation and still say the cosmos at large is not a mind at all. Kastrup argues the case hard and it hangs together, but it is an interpretation of reality, taken up As-If, not a thing shown true from the outside. And there is an honest crack even inside the theory, which we flag plainly: saying “it’s all one universal mind” does not actually explain why there is experience at all — it just moves the mystery. Instead of asking how brains make feeling, you now must ask why mind-at-large feels anything in the first place. The hard problem may not be dissolved so much as relocated to the bottom of everything, where it is harder to see. That is a real limit, not a footnote.

And now the fault line that earns the next page. You just left Hoffman, who agreed with Kastrup on the biggest thing of all — that consciousness is the ground, and matter is the derived appearance. They stand together there, against nearly all of science. But they split on one word, and it is the same split that has run through this whole book: how many? Hoffman said many — a crowd of separate conscious agents, plurality all the way down. Kastrup says that cannot be the bottom; underneath the crowd there is one, and the separateness is dissociation, a wall the single mind throws up inside itself. Sea, not crowd.

The thinker you meet next, Philip Goff, disagrees with both of them on something even more basic. Kastrup says only mind is real, and matter is nothing but appearance — a mask, an outside view, with no substance of its own behind the looking. Goff will not go that far. Goff is a panpsychist: he says matter is perfectly real, real stuff really out there — but that this real matter has experience built into it, all the way down, so that even the smallest bits of the physical world carry a flicker of feeling. For Kastrup, matter is a derived appearance of mind; for Goff, matter is a fundamental thing that possesses mind. One says the physical world is a picture with nothing physical behind it; the other says the physical world is solidly there and conscious to its core. That is a sharp, real quarrel between two people who both refuse to call the universe dead — and it is why each gets a page of their own. Turn it, and meet the one who keeps the matter and puts the mind inside it.
