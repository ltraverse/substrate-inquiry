References & Acknowledgements

Every voice in this book, and where to hear it in its own words.

This is a draft, held As-If — an exploration, not a settled claim. Each thinker is quoted briefly, under fair use, and every quoted passage is short and attributed. Where a page paraphrases rather than quotes, it says so. The primary works below are the honest floor: if a page moved you, go read the source, not the summary.

Part I · The Scroll — thinkers of the past

The past thinkers speak through classic, public-domain works (given here in their standard forms and translations).

  • The Authors of the Upanishads — The Principal Upanishads (esp. the Māṇḍūkya, Bṛhadāraṇyaka, and Chāndogya), c. 800–200 BCE.
  • The Buddha — the Pāli Canon, esp. the Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta (the discourse on not-self), Saṃyutta Nikāya, c. 5th century BCE.
  • Socrates & Plato — Plato, Meno and Phaedo (the theory of recollection), c. 385–380 BCE.
  • Adi Shankara — Vivekachūḍāmaṇi and the commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, c. 8th century CE.
  • René Descartes — Meditations on First Philosophy (1641); Discourse on the Method (1637).
  • John Locke — An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689).
  • Immanuel Kant — Critique of Pure Reason (1781, revised 1787).
  • William James — The Principles of Psychology (1890).
  • Sigmund Freud — The Interpretation of Dreams (1899/1900); The Ego and the Id (1923).
  • David Chalmers — “Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness,” Journal of Consciousness Studies 2(3): 200–219 (1995); The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory, Oxford University Press (1996).

Part II · The Screen — thinkers of the present

Living thinkers, cited to the peer-reviewed paper or book where the claim is stated with its full rigor and limits.

  • Donald Hoffman — Hoffman & Prakash, “Objects of Consciousness,” Frontiers in Psychology 5:577 (2014); The Case Against Reality, W. W. Norton (2019).
  • Bernardo Kastrup — Kastrup, Crabtree & Kelly, “Could Multiple Personality Disorder Explain Life, the Universe and Everything?” Scientific American (2018); The Idea of the World, iff Books (2019).
  • Philip Goff — “The Case for Panpsychism,” Philosophy Now 121 (2017); Galileo’s Error, Pantheon (2019); Consciousness and Fundamental Reality, Oxford University Press (2017).
  • Giulio Tononi — Oizumi, Albantakis & Tononi, “Integrated Information Theory 3.0,” PLoS Computational Biology 10(5):e1003588 (2014); Tononi, Boly, Massimini & Koch, Nature Reviews Neuroscience 17:450–461 (2016); Phi: A Voyage from the Brain to the Soul, Pantheon (2012).
  • Roger Penrose & Stuart Hameroff — Penrose, Shadows of the Mind, Oxford University Press (1994; the Gödelian argument, p. 76); The Emperor’s New Mind (1989); Hameroff & Penrose, “Consciousness in the universe: A review of the Orch OR theory,” Physics of Life Reviews 11(1):39–78 (2014).
  • Karl Friston — “The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?” Nature Reviews Neuroscience 11:127–138 (2010).
  • Anil Seth — Being You: A New Science of Consciousness, Faber / Dutton (2021); “Your brain hallucinates your conscious reality,” TED2017.
  • Michael Levin — “Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere (TAME),” Frontiers in Systems Neuroscience 16:768201 (2022); Levin & Dennett, “Cognition all the way down,” Aeon (2020).
  • Keith Frankish — “Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness,” Journal of Consciousness Studies 23(11–12):11–39 (2016); ed., Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness, Imprint Academic (2017).
  • Chris Fields — Fields, Hoffman, Prakash & Singh, “Conscious agent networks,” Cognitive Systems Research 47:186–213 (2018); Fields, “Building the observer into the system” (2016).
