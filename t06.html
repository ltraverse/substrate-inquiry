The Scroll · Six

John Locke

You reach only as far as your consciousness reaches back.

John Locke was an English doctor and philosopher writing in the 1600s, a careful, practical man who trusted plain observation over grand systems. Where the last thinker, Descartes, went hunting for one thing he could not doubt, Locke asks a question you could put to a jury: what makes the person standing here today the same person who did something years ago? It sounds obvious until you push on it — and when you push, everything you’d reach for first falls away. This page walks his answer one slow step at a time. It is a quiet idea, but it changed how we think about who we are, and it still runs under our courtrooms and our arguments about blame. Take it gently.

Start with a question you already answer every day

Someone asks: “Are you the same person who broke that window when you were seven?” You say yes without thinking. But why yes? What is it that stays the same across all those years, so that the child and the grown-up count as one person and not two?

Try the obvious answers, and watch each one crumble.

Is it the same body? It can’t be. Your body is not one fixed thing — it is a river. The cells swap out, the atoms you’re made of now were somewhere else a few years ago, the seven-year-old’s body is physically gone. If sameness of person meant sameness of stuff, you’d be a stranger to your own childhood.

Is it the same soul — some hidden spiritual substance under it all? Locke’s move here is bold and honest: even if there is such a soul-stuff, it can’t be what makes you you. Suppose that same soul-substance had lived a whole earlier life you cannot remember a scrap of — some ancient person, gone from you completely. Would you be responsible for what they did? Of course not. You’d feel no more tied to them than to a stranger. So sameness of the underlying stuff, body or soul, is not what we actually mean when we say “the same person.” Something else is doing the work.

Locke’s answer: the thread is consciousness

Here is his idea, and it is startlingly simple. What makes you the same person over time is consciousness — by which he means “the perception of what passes in a man’s own mind.” Your awareness of your own thoughts and deeds. The fact that you can turn back and find a past thought or action and know it as yours.

You are the same person as far back as your consciousness can reach and take hold of a past moment — “and no further.” Those three words do enormous work. The self does not stretch as far as your body, or your soul, or the calendar. It stretches exactly as far as your consciousness can extend backward and lay claim to what it finds there.

Notice what this makes the self. Not a lump of matter, not a spark of soul-stuff — but a reach. A living thread of awareness running back through time, gathering up the moments it can still own. You are the length of that thread.

The prince and the cobbler

Locke had a way of testing an idea by imagining something wild, to see which way our own judgment jumps. So picture this. One night the entire consciousness of a prince — all his memories, his sense of himself, everything he can look back on and call mine — leaves his body and wakes up inside the body of a cobbler, a poor shoemaker, while the cobbler’s own consciousness is gone. The prince’s body lies there, empty of that inner life.

Now: who is the person walking around in the cobbler’s body?

Everyone, Locke says, feels the answer at once. It is the prince — the same person, wearing the wrong body. He remembers the palace, owns the prince’s deeds, calls the prince’s life his own. The person went with the consciousness, not with the body. And here is the sharp edge: if the person goes with the consciousness, then the body is just clothing. The self is not the thing you’re made of. It’s the awareness that reaches back and says “that was me.”

The self is not a thing — it is an act

This is the heart of it, so go slowly. We usually picture the self as a thing you have — a little core inside you, sitting there, being you. Locke quietly replaces that picture with a doing.

The self, for him, is an act of appropriation. “Appropriation” just means claiming something as your own — the way you’d put your name on a bag. Consciousness reaches out across your past, touches a thought here, a deed there, and folds them in: mine, mine, that too is mine. That gathering-in, that binding of scattered moments into one owner, is the self. There is no owner sitting behind the act, waiting; the owning is the owner. You are not a thing that happens to remember. You are the remembering, doing its work of claiming.

Where all of it comes from: the white paper

Step back once more and ask where the mind gets anything to be conscious of. Locke’s famous answer sits under this whole picture. The mind, at the start, is like white paper — blank, no writing on it, no ideas stamped in before experience. (He wrote against people who thought we’re born already knowing certain truths; he saw no such thing in a newborn.)

So how does the paper get written on? Through two channels, and only two.

The first is sensation — your outer senses. Sight, sound, touch, taste, smell carry the world in and leave their marks. That’s the obvious one.

The second is quieter and, for our story, the important one: reflection. This is an inner sense — the mind watching its own operations. Not seeing the world, but noticing itself seeing; catching itself thinking, doubting, wanting, remembering. Reflection is the mind turned back on its own activity. And that turning-back is exactly the material a self is made of, because the self is consciousness owning its own past acts. The inner sense that watches the mind is the same power by which the mind claims itself across time.

Why he calls “person” a courtroom word

One last piece, and it locks the whole thing together. Locke says flatly that “person” is a forensic term — a word built for the courtroom, for handing out praise and blame, reward and punishment.

Sit with why that matters. If we only ever punish and reward persons, and a person is defined by what consciousness can reach and own, then it follows that we can only justly hold you to account for what you can actually own as yours. This is not a soft idea — it’s a hard, practical one. It ties the whole self to a real limit: the reach of your own consciousness. What you cannot reach back to and claim, you are not — and so, in the deepest sense, it is not yours to answer for. Locke built the self out of exactly the stuff that a fair judgment would need it to be built from.

The nugget

You are not the body you’re made of, or some hidden soul-stuff underneath. You are a reach of consciousness — a living thread of awareness that stretches back through time, gathering up the moments it can still own and binding them into one owner. You are as far back as you can reach and say “that was me,” and no further.

To know this is to see the self as something done, not something had — and to feel, gently, where its edges are.

Hold it honestly

Hold it As-If, not As-Proven, and notice first where Locke and the man on the page before him part ways — because the disagreement is the whole lesson.

Descartes made the self a substance — a thinking thing that simply exists, whole and continuous, whether or not it remembers a moment of its past. For Descartes, you are; the self is a solid fact, and it needed God to hold it steady across time. Locke pointedly refuses this. He lifts the self off any substance and sets it down on a relation — the reach of memory-consciousness. That is a genuine break. For Descartes the self is a thing that has thoughts; for Locke the self just is the thread of owned thoughts, and nothing more solid underneath. Where Descartes leaned on God to bind the self together, Locke lets it be bound — or broken — by consciousness alone.

And here is the unsettling place that break leads, which you can test against your own sense of fairness. If the self reaches only as far as consciousness reaches, then cut the reach — forget completely — and the deed drops out of the self. The part of your life you can no longer reach back to and own is, for Locke, strictly not you. That is either a profound insight or a real problem, and philosophers have argued it ever since: it seems to say a man who has genuinely, totally forgotten a crime is not the same person who did it — which our courts do not accept. Locke saw the sting and half-embraced it; that’s the daring of the idea and its exposed nerve at once.

What is solidly checkable: the plain observation Locke starts from — that your body and its matter change out entirely, so bodily stuff cannot be what your sameness rests on. Reflection, too, you can feel in a second: turn your attention onto your own thinking and catch the mind watching itself. That inner sense is real. What is the leap is the claim that consciousness is all there is to being the same person — that with the memory gone, the person is gone. That you cannot prove from the outside; you can only weigh it against how it feels to lose a memory, and against what justice seems to require. Two careful men stood at the same self and built it from opposite materials — one a substance, one a reach. That they disagree is not a flaw to iron out. It is the shape of how hard it is to say what you are.

“…person… is a thinking intelligent being, that has reason and reflection, and can consider itself as itself, the same thinking thing, in different times and places; which it does only by that consciousness which is inseparable from thinking… As far as this consciousness can be extended backwards to any past action or thought, so far reaches the identity of that person.” — John Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding, II.xxvii.9
