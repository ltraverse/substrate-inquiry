The Screen · Eight

Michael Levin

Mind goes all the way down.

Every thinker so far has looked for mind in the usual place — in a person, behind a pair of eyes, inside a skull. Michael Levin is a biologist who studies something much smaller and much stranger, and he came to a conclusion that turns the usual place inside out. He didn’t get there by philosophy. He got there by cutting up flatworms, growing frogs with eyes in odd places, and watching what living cells actually do when you leave them alone. This page walks you there one slow step at a time, from a worm in a dish to a claim about where mind begins. Go gently; each step is a small, checkable fact, and only at the end do the small facts add up to something large.

Start with a worm you cut in half

There is a small flatworm called a planarian. It lives in ponds, it is about the length of a grain of rice, and it has a head end and a tail end, like most animals.

Now do something to it that would end almost anything else: cut it in half, straight across the middle.

It does not die. The head half grows a new tail. And the tail half — the piece with no brain, no eyes, nothing you would call a head — grows a new head, with a new brain and new eyes, exactly where a head should go. Not a second tail. Not a lump. A head. Cut the worm into a dozen pieces and you get a dozen worms, each one rebuilding precisely the parts it is missing and stopping precisely when the shape is right.

Sit with the strange part. Each piece somehow knows what a whole worm is supposed to look like, notices which bits are absent, builds exactly those, and then — this is the key — stops. How does a headless scrap of tissue know the difference between “not done yet” and “done”? Something in there is holding a picture of the finished worm and working to close the gap between what-is and what-should-be. Hold that phrase — closing the gap toward a goal — because it is the whole idea, and it is about to grow.

A cell has somewhere it is trying to go

Levin’s move is to take that phrase seriously and ask: what is the smallest thing that has a goal?

Zoom all the way down, to a single cell. A cell is not a brain. It has no thoughts. But watch one: it senses what is around it — this chemical is thicker over here, thinner over there — and it moves toward what it needs and away from what harms it. If you block its path, it goes around. If the usual route is closed, it finds another. It is not just being pushed by physics like a rock rolling downhill. It is getting somewhere it is trying to get.

Levin gives this a careful name: the cell is solving problems in a problem-space. That is a plain idea dressed in two words. A problem-space is just the range of situations a thing can be in, plus the place it is trying to reach. For a mouse in a maze, the problem-space is the maze and the goal is the cheese. For a single cell, the problem-space is chemistry — the shifting soup around it — and its goal is the good spot in that soup. Same shape of behavior — steer toward the goal, route around obstacles — just a smaller, humbler arena.

And here is the careful, load-bearing word he chooses for what the cell is doing: not “thinking,” not “feeling,” but competency — the plain ability to reach a goal, and to still reach it when the path changes. He is not saying the cell has an inner life. He is saying it has aims and the skill to pursue them, and that this is not poetry — it is what the cell measurably does.

Selves stack — and can come apart

Now the step that makes it a theory of us, and not just of worms and cells.

A cell has a small goal, in a small problem-space: chemistry, right around itself. But cells join up. And when they do, something new appears. A sheet of cells building an organ is no longer navigating chemistry — together they are navigating anatomy, a far bigger problem-space, steering toward a goal no single cell could hold: the shape of the whole body. The planarian’s tail-scrap rebuilding a head is a crowd of cells sharing one goal-picture that none of them owns alone.

So selves stack. Little selves with little goals cooperate into a bigger self with a bigger goal, and that bigger self is as real as the small ones — it is just working in a larger space. You are the top of such a stack: trillions of cells, each still minding its own small business, bound into one self that has goals — a career, a promise, a book — no cell could dream of. Levin’s word for this is a nested self: selves inside selves, each scale with its own reach.

And a stack can come apart — which is the eeriest evidence that it was ever really stacked. Think about what cancer is in this picture. A cancer cell is not an invader from outside. It is one of your own cells whose sense of “self” has shrunk — it has stopped taking part in the big goal (build and maintain this body) and fallen back to the oldest, smallest goal there is: make more of me, now, here. It seceded. The large self lost a citizen back down to the single-cell scale. That tells you the large self was a genuine union all along — because it can suffer a defection.

The turn — a self is a loop that holds itself together

So what actually is one of these selves, at any scale? Here is Levin’s deepest move, and it is worth going slowly.

A self, he says, is not a thing or a place. It is a loop. A cell collective keeps a shared memory of the goal — what the finished body should be — and it does this partly with bioelectricity: cells talk to each other with small electrical voltages, the same kind of signaling neurons use, but running in ordinary tissue long before there is any brain. That electrical pattern is a map of the target shape, held across the whole collective, that the cells keep reading and keep working to match.

Levin proved this is a real, editable map, not a metaphor. His lab changed the electrical pattern in flatworm tissue — left the genes untouched — and grew a worm with two heads. Change the map, change the body the cells build toward. He built creatures called Xenobots: he took ordinary skin cells from a frog embryo, freed them from the big frog-goal, and on their own they regrouped into a tiny new being that swims and moves and heals itself — cells writing a smaller self when released from the larger one.

Now the loop. A self is a control system that senses where it is, compares that to where it is trying to be, and acts to close the gap — and part of what it is trying to preserve is the loop itself. It works to keep on being the thing that has this goal. Levin’s striking way of putting it: a self “locks itself into existence.” It is not held together from outside, by a soul poured in or a boss cell giving orders. It holds itself together, by continuously caring about its own goal and defending its own boundary — the line between “me, pursuing this” and “everything else.” Selfhood is not a substance. It is a self-referential loop that keeps re-drawing the border of the self it is.

That is why mind, in Levin’s picture, goes all the way down. Not because a cell secretly thinks like you — it doesn’t — but because the same shape — a loop drawing a boundary, holding a goal, closing a gap, defending its own existence — is there in the cell, in the tissue, in the organ, and in you. It is not that big minds are made of tiny minds. It is that “having a mind” was never one special thing that switched on at the brain. It is a dial, turned low in a cell and high in you, present at every scale in between.

The nugget

Mind is not a special thing that begins at the brain. It is what any living self does at any size: draw a boundary, hold a goal, and work to close the gap between what-is and what-should-be. Cells do it in chemistry, tissues do it in anatomy, you do it in a whole life. Selves stack — little goals cooperating into bigger ones — and a self is not a substance but a loop that holds itself together by caring about its own aim. Cognition goes all the way down, not as a claim that a cell can feel, but as the plain fact that goal-seeking, and the self that seeks, appear at every scale of life.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, not As-Proven — and here the honest work, as with the last few pages, is to cut the claim cleanly in two, because Levin himself is unusually careful about the cut.

What is genuinely real is the biology, and it is astonishing. Planarians do regrow exactly the right anatomy and stop at the right place. Bioelectric patterns are a memory of target shape you can measure, and Levin did rewrite one to grow a two-headed worm without touching a gene, and Xenobots do self-assemble and move. This is laboratory work, published and repeated, that could have failed and didn’t. The strong, unspoiled point it establishes: goal-directed problem-solving is not the private property of brains — it runs through living matter at every scale, and treating a cell or a tissue as a small agent with aims makes real, testable predictions that treating it as a dumb machine does not. That much stands, and it is a genuine shift in how biology is done.

What is the leap is the word — calling this “cognition,” or “mind.” Levin is scrupulous here, and it matters: his claim is about competency and goal-directedness, the measurable steering-toward-a-goal, and he stays agnostic about whether any felt experience — anything it is like to be that cell — is present at the low scales. The biology tells you the cell pursues a goal; it does not tell you the cell feels anything doing it. That second thing — the inner light, the felt inside that this whole book circles — is exactly what the experiments cannot reach, and Levin, to his credit, does not pretend they do. So take the science at full strength and hold the mind-word loosely: “cognition all the way down” is, honestly read, goal-seeking all the way down, with the question of felt experience left open.

And that open question is the fault line that earns the next page. The thinker who follows, Keith Frankish, comes at it from the opposite end — and hard. Levin finds real, if minimal, minds reaching down into the smallest living things. Frankish, an “illusionist,” says the arrow points the other way: that even our own rich inner experience — the vivid felt “something it is like” we are all so sure of — is itself a kind of user-illusion the brain runs on itself, not the bedrock it seems. If Frankish is right that our felt mind is partly a trick of self-modeling, then a cell’s “cognition” is at most function — real goal-seeking, yes, but with no felt anything anywhere, top or bottom. So the two of them frame the sharpest version of the whole book’s question: Levin says mind goes all the way down; Frankish says the felt part of mind may not fully exist even at the top. One says there is more mind in the world than we thought. The other says there is less. Turn the page and meet the one who says less.
