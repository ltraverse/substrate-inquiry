The Screen · Six

Karl Friston

The self is the story a body tells to keep itself alive.

So far the living thinkers have come at the mystery from evolution, from physics, from the strange math of many minds. Karl Friston comes at it from the least likely place of all: the equations a brain scientist uses to describe how a body stays alive. He is one of the most cited neuroscientists alive, and he built a single idea so broad it tries to explain, in one stroke, why anything living holds together at all — and, along the way, what a “self” even is. The idea has hard-sounding names (free energy, Markov blankets) that scare people off. Don’t let them. Underneath, it is one simple, almost stubborn thought, and we are going to walk to it one small step at a time, starting from a cube of ice.

Start with something falling apart

Put an ice cube on a warm table and walk away. Come back in an hour: a puddle. Come back in a day: dry — the water has spread into the air, gone.

This is the most ordinary thing in the world, and also the deepest law we know. Left alone, everything spreads out. The hot coffee goes cold. The sandcastle slumps back into flat sand. The drop of ink uncurls through the glass of water until it’s the palest, evenest grey. Nothing ever does the reverse on its own — you have never once seen a cold cup of coffee reheat itself, or grey water sort back into clear water and a tidy drop of ink. The universe has a direction, and the direction is toward mush. Toward mixed, spread-out, shapeless sameness.

Scientists have a name for how spread-out and mixed something is: entropy. High entropy just means “well and truly mush.” And the law is simply this: entropy goes up. Things fall apart. Order leaks away into disorder, always, everywhere, given time.

Now hold your own hand up and look at it.

The strange thing that refuses to spread

Your hand is not spreading into the air. Neither are you. You have kept roughly the same shape, the same warmth, the same you-ness, for years — while the ink drop next to you wouldn’t last a minute. A living body is the one thing in the room that seems to be breaking the rule, holding its order together while everything around it slides toward mush.

It isn’t really breaking the rule — it pays for its order by making a bigger mess outside (heat, waste, spent food). But the fact stays astonishing: a living thing is a pocket of order that keeps rebuilding itself faster than the world can wear it down. It is, you could say, a standing bet against decay. Every second it is quietly refusing to become a puddle.

Friston’s whole theory begins by taking that refusal seriously and asking: what must a thing do, mechanically, to keep pulling it off? Not “why is life special” in a misty way — but what is the actual job description of staying alive? And his answer is the seed of everything: to keep existing, a thing has to keep itself in the small handful of states where it still works — and stay out of all the countless states where it comes apart.

A fish works in water and comes apart on dry land. Your body works at about 37 degrees and comes apart at 45. Out of the billion ways your insides could be arranged, only a narrow few are “alive.” To persist is to keep winning your way back to that narrow few, over and over, against a world forever trying to scatter you across all the rest.

Surprise — the one word the whole theory turns on

Now Friston gives that narrow band of “working” states a name, and it’s a name worth slowing down for, because it sounds like it means one thing and actually means another.

He calls the bad states — the ones where you’re coming apart — surprising.

Not surprising like a birthday party. Surprising in a precise sense: a state you almost never find yourself in, if you’re the kind of thing you are. A fish on a riverbank is in a hugely “surprising” state — fish are almost never there, because fish that end up there stop being fish. A body at 45 degrees is a “surprising” state for a body. Being intact, fed, and breathing is unsurprising — it’s exactly where a healthy creature spends nearly all its time. So flip it around and you get Friston’s core move:

To stay alive is to avoid surprising states. To persist is, quite literally, to keep your own surprise low.

That’s the whole engine. “Free energy,” the scary phrase in his title, is at bottom just a measurable stand-in for surprise — a quantity a system can actually compute and push down, because raw surprise itself is too slippery to measure directly. When you read “minimize free energy,” you can honestly hear “keep surprise low,” which means “stay in the states where you keep existing.” The math is hard; the meaning is not.

To avoid surprise, you have to guess

Here is the step that turns a survival rule into a theory of mind. Go slowly.

If your job is to avoid states that would break you, you can’t wait until you’re already in one — by then you’ve broken. You have to see it coming. You have to guess what’s about to happen and get out of the way first. The creature that can predict “the shadow overhead usually means a hawk” and duck stays unsurprising; the one that only reacts once the talons are in gets a very surprising afternoon.

So anything that keeps itself alive has to carry, somewhere inside, a working guess about its world — a set of expectations for what its senses are about to report. Friston calls this a model. Not a conscious one, not words — just the built-in bet a system makes about what comes next. And staying alive turns into a loop with two ways to win:

  • Change the guess to fit the world. Your senses report something your model didn’t expect (a wet foot where you predicted dry). You update the guess. That updating — we usually call it perceiving, and learning.
  • Change the world to fit the guess. Or you can act — move your body so the world lines up with what you predicted. You expected your foot to be dry, so you pull it out of the puddle and make it dry. Friston calls this active inference: you don’t just passively predict the world, you nudge the world into matching your prediction.

Sit with how strange and lovely that second one is. On this view, an action is not separate from a thought. Reaching for a cup is your body confirming a prediction — “there will be a cup in my hand” — by making it true. Perceiving and acting become the same one activity, run in two directions: bring the map to the world, or bring the world to the map, whichever keeps surprise low. That single loop, Friston argues, is what a nervous system is for.

The turn — the boundary, and the story it tells

We have a thing that persists by guessing and acting. Two questions are left, and they are the ones that put Friston in a book about consciousness. Where does the thing stop and the world begin? And who, in all this, is the “self” doing the guessing?

Start with the edge. For any of this to work — for there to be an “inside” that models an “outside” — there has to be a boundary between them: a surface where the world touches the system and the system touches back. Everything the inside can ever know of the outside has to cross that surface; the inside never touches the world directly, only the reports arriving at its own border. Friston borrows a name for this boundary from mathematics: a Markov blanket. Picture a bubble around the living thing, made not of soap but of sensing (what comes in) and acting (what goes out). Inside the bubble: you. Outside: the world. And the only truth about the world you will ever have is what is written on the inside face of that bubble. (Keep this bubble in mind — much later, in The Glass, this exact boundary turns out to be the thing several of our thinkers were circling from different sides.)

Now the last and sharpest question. If the self is sealed inside a bubble, only ever reading its own inner surface, then what is the self? Friston’s answer is bracing, and it is the reason his page carries the epigram it does. The self, he says, is not a little extra thing living in the bubble — not a soul, not a driver, not a spark tucked behind the eyes. The self is simply the model the system keeps of itself in order to go on predicting well and staying alive. It is the body’s own best guess about what it is, held because a thing that guesses what it is can guess what it needs and where the danger lies. Friston has put it as bluntly as it can be put: the self is, in the end, just a story — a story the system tells, and keeps telling, because telling it is part of how the system stays in one piece.

Not a story in the sense of “false.” A story in the sense of authored, ongoing, and for a purpose. You are the narrative your body maintains about itself so that it can keep being a body. Stop telling the story — stop modelling, stop predicting, stop pulling yourself back from the surprising states — and you don’t become a freed spirit. You become the puddle. The self, on this view, is not the passenger. It is the act of holding together, seen from the inside.

The nugget

A living thing is a bet against decay: to keep existing it must stay out of the states that would dissolve it, and to do that it must guess what is coming and act to make its guesses true. The boundary where its guessing meets the world is its edge; and the “self” is not a thing behind that edge but the story the system tells about itself in order to keep the bet alive. You are what it feels like from the inside to be a piece of the world refusing, moment by moment, to come apart.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, not As-Proven — and here the honest work is to separate two things that Friston’s own framework tends to fuse: a genuine piece of testable science, and a large metaphysical reading laid on top of it.

What is real and checkable is the machinery. That brains work by prediction — that they carry expectations, register the gap when the world defies them, and both learn and act to close that gap — is not mysticism; it is a live, productive research program that has made specific, falsifiable predictions about how real neurons and real perception behave, and much of it has held up. As a working model of what a nervous system is doing, active inference earns its keep.

Where the ground gets soft is the size of the claim. The Free Energy Principle is stated so generally that it reaches to cover a cell, a brain, a forest, arguably a rock — anything that persists. That sweep is thrilling, and it is also the problem: a statement that fits everything that stays in one piece starts to look less like a discovery about the world and more like a re-description of what “staying in one piece” means — true almost by definition. Serious critics press exactly here: if no possible observation could count against it, is it a theory, or a very elegant tautology? The honest reader keeps the checkable neuroscience and treats the grand “principle of all persisting things” as a frame held As-If, not a proven law.

And there is a quieter limit that matters most for this book. Even granting every word — that you are a self-modelling bubble minimizing surprise — the theory is silent on why any of it is felt. It can tell you, in beautiful detail, how a system tracks and corrects and predicts. It does not tell you why there is something it is like to be that system rather than nothing at all. Friston, to his credit, largely stays on the instrument’s side of that line: the self as model, the self as story, a description of function that need not commit to any inner glow.

Which is precisely the fault line that earns the next page. Anil Seth takes Friston’s whole prediction machine and refuses to leave it cold. Building directly on this foundation, Seth argues the body’s predictions are not just bookkeeping — they are genuinely felt, that feeling itself is the brain’s controlled guess about the state of its own flesh, and that we are, in his phrase, “beast machines” for whom the predicting is the experiencing. Where Friston is content to call the self a useful story, Seth insists the story is lived from the inside. Same machine; one man reads it as instrument, the next as the very stuff of feeling. Turn the page and meet the one who says the prediction is felt.

“The free-energy principle says that any self-organizing system that is at equilibrium with its environment must minimize its free energy. The principle is essentially a mathematical formulation of how adaptive systems (that is, biological agents, like animals or brains) resist a natural tendency to disorder.” — Karl Friston, “The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?”, Nature Reviews Neuroscience 11, 127–138 (2010).
