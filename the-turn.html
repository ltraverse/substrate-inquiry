The Turn

Àlex Gómez-Marín

Put the observer back into the method.

You have just met twenty thinkers, and every one of them was answering the same question: what is consciousness? This page does something different, and that is why it stands alone, between the last of them and the view this book will finally offer. Àlex Gómez-Marín is a working scientist — a theoretical physicist who became a neuroscientist and now runs a laboratory that studies how living things behave. He does not hand you a twenty-first answer. He asks a harder, quieter question, the one hiding underneath all twenty: how are we even allowed to study this — and what did science quietly throw away to be allowed? Take this page slowly. It is the hinge the whole book turns on.

Start with something in your own mouth

Bite into a slice of lemon.

There is the sourness — sharp, bright, unmistakable. Now here is the strange thing. That sourness is the single most certain thing in your whole world right now. You cannot doubt it; it is simply, undeniably there. And yet it is the one thing no instrument in the world can reach. A scientist could wire your tongue, scan your brain, film your face puckering — and gather a perfect record of the machinery of tasting. But the sourness itself, the way it is for you, would be nowhere in all that data. You have it completely. Their instruments have none of it.

Hold both halves of that at once, because the whole page lives here: the sourness is the most certain thing you know, and it is invisible to the outside look. The thing you are surest of is the thing science, as normally practised, cannot see.

Gómez-Marín’s life’s work begins by refusing to shrug this off. Most scientists treat that sourness — your raw, first-person experience — as a fog to be cleared away, a private curtain in front of the “real” data. He says the exact opposite. That sourness is not the curtain. It is the ground everything else is standing on.

The bargain science made — and forgot it made

To see why, go back to how science won.

Science became powerful by making one brilliant deal with itself: only count what two people can check. If you say the water is boiling, I can put a thermometer in and read the same number you read. If you say the star is that bright, we can both point the same instrument at it and agree. Your private feelings, your hunches, your inner life — those were set aside, because I cannot check your insides. Only the public, the measurable, the third-person “view from nowhere” was allowed to count as evidence. Call it what Gómez-Marín calls it: the cult of objectivity.

And it worked. It gave us the entire triumph of physics and chemistry and biology — stars, atoms, genes, the whole shining machine. When you are studying stones and stars, throwing out the inner life costs you nothing, because stones have no inner life to throw out.

But watch what happens when that same science, flushed with three centuries of victory, finally turns around and points itself at you — at the taster, the one having the experience. It arrives carrying its old rule in its hand: your inner experience doesn’t count as evidence. And so, at the very moment it tries to study consciousness, it has already thrown consciousness out of the room. It excludes the one thing it was supposed to explain — before it even begins.

This is the sentence to slow down on: experience is the thing that makes science possible in the first place — there is no observation, no data, no reading of any dial, without someone experiencing it — and yet science has built a method with no room for experience inside it. It kept the fruit and cut down the tree. Gómez-Marín’s word for the whole situation is plain: consciousness science has been broken by the cult of objectivity.

The trick hidden in the question

Here is how the break hides itself, so well that most scientists never notice they are inside it.

Listen to the question the field asks, the official one, printed in the textbooks: “How does the brain produce consciousness?” It sounds neutral. It sounds like a good, hard scientific problem. But feel what has been slipped inside it. It has already decided that the brain does produce consciousness — that matter comes first and experience is something matter manufactures. The word “how” smuggles in the whole answer and disguises it as a question.

Gómez-Marín compares it to a courtroom. Imagine being asked, “How did you kill him?” There is no innocent way to answer. Say anything at all about the how and you have already confessed to the whether. The only free move is to step back and say: wait — you haven’t shown that I did. The honest question was never “how does the brain produce consciousness.” It was “does it — and how would we know?” A science that forgets to ask whether has stopped being science about that thing and started being a belief defending itself.

And here is his most cutting historical claim, the one that names this whole part of the book. Thirty years ago, when the “hard problem of consciousness” was given its famous name, everyone treated it as a tough puzzle science would eventually crack. Gómez-Marín reads it the other way. Calling it a hard problem — a thing still to be solved — was a way of not saying the thing the evidence was actually showing: that the old picture, matter-first, experience-as-afterthought, had quietly died. Naming it “hard” instead of “finished” gave the dying picture, in his words, a loan for another hundred years. The label bought time for a paradigm that had already run out.

The turn

So what do you do about it? This is the move that makes Gómez-Marín the hinge of this book, and it is beautifully simple to state.

You just met Chris Fields, who proved something exact and humbling: standing outside a thing, reading only its surface, you can never get behind the wall to whether there is anyone home. From the outside, the felt inside is sealed. Fields is right. But look at what he assumed without saying it — what everyone in the cult of objectivity assumes: that the outside is the only place a scientist is allowed to stand.

Gómez-Marín points at the one exception in the entire universe. There is exactly one wall you are not stuck outside of. Your own. You do not have to infer your sourness from the outside; you are the inside. The first person is not a weakness to be scrubbed out of science — it is the single place where the sealed box is open, because you are the box, looking out.

So his proposal is not a new theory of consciousness. It is a repair to the method: put the observer back in. Build a science that is allowed to take first-person experience as real data — carefully, rigorously, but as evidence, not fog. He calls it first-person science, and he argues for it not from a meditation cushion but from inside peer-reviewed neuroscience, in its own journals, in its own language. This is not “stop doing science.” It is “stop doing a science that forbids its own foundation.”

Notice what he refuses to do next. He will not plant a flag and name his own grand answer — he half-jokingly calls his position the un-theory of consciousness. And that refusal is exactly why he stands here and not back among the twenty. He is not offering a twenty-first lens to argue with the other twenty. He is telling you why the whole gallery of lenses has been so unable to settle anything: they were all built by a method that had thrown away the evidence that could settle it.

The nugget

Science became powerful by trusting only what two people can check from the outside — and it was right to, for stones and stars. But experience is what makes every observation possible in the first place, and there is exactly one place in the universe where you are not stuck outside the wall: your own. A science that studies consciousness while forbidding first-person experience as evidence has excluded the one thing it was trying to explain. Put the observer back into the method.

Hold it honestly

This book holds everything As-If, and this page needs that discipline most of all — because it is the easiest one to over-trust and the easiest one to wave away, and both are mistakes.

What is solid is the methodology critique, and it is very solid. It survives the hardest test we put to any idea in this book: it would hold even for someone who wanted it to be false. You do not have to believe consciousness is fundamental to admit that a method built to ignore inner experience is a strange tool for studying inner experience. The point is argued in peer-reviewed neuroscience, not smuggled in from the fringe. That much is load-bearing, and it is why Gómez-Marín earns his place: he is the inquiry’s cleanest witness from inside the credentialed field for the one move the whole book rests on — that the observer belongs in the account, not outside it.

What is not settled — and honesty demands we say it plainly — is the further, bolder work he also does: experiments on perception without the eyes, on knowing-at-a-distance, even on knowing before. These are real experiments he genuinely runs, not idle talk, and that courage is part of why he matters. But by his own admission they are unfinished, contested, and thin on confirmed results. This page does not rest on any of them. It rests only on the method critique — the part that stands whether or not a single one of those experiments ever pans out. Keep those two things apart, exactly as he asks us to keep the observer and the observed apart: take the repair to the method as strong, and hold the frontier experiments as open questions, no more.

There is a test he offers that belongs in this book like a key in a lock. When a fellow scientist told him a claim was simply impossible, he did not argue the claim. He asked one question: what is your prior — before any evidence — that this could be true? If it is exactly zero, then no evidence could ever move you, and we are not doing science anymore; we are defending a faith. That is the same discipline that runs underneath this entire inquiry — and it cuts both ways, which is the only honest way to hold a blade. A prior of zero against a claim is a closed mind wearing a lab coat. But a prior of one — certainty for a claim you want to be true — is the same closed mind, facing the other direction. Gómez-Marín keeps both open at once, and asks us to.

And now the doorway. Fields showed you a wall you can never get behind from outside. Gómez-Marín turns and says: then stop pretending outside is the only place to stand — you are one wall’s inside, and that is not a flaw in your evidence, it is your evidence. What would it look like to take that seriously — to build a picture of the world from the one window that opens inward? Turn the page. That picture is The Glass.
