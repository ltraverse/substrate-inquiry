The Screen · Four

Giulio Tononi

Consciousness is how much of you can’t be split apart.

Every thinker so far has circled the mystery with words — the witness that can’t catch itself, the felt inside no machine explains. Giulio Tononi is a psychiatrist and neuroscientist who tried to do something almost nobody had dared: turn consciousness into a number. Not to explain it away, but to measure it — to say of any object in the world, this much conscious, that much, none at all. His theory is called Integrated Information Theory, and it is the hardest page in this book, because at its heart is a piece of mathematics. So we will go slowly, from a thing in your pocket to a claim about what experience actually is. Every step is small. Take them one at a time.

Start with the camera in your phone

Your phone camera has, say, twelve million tiny light sensors, packed in a grid behind the lens. Point it at a sunset. Each sensor does exactly one job: it measures the light landing on its own little spot and reports a number — this bright, this red. Twelve million sensors, twelve million separate little reports.

Here is the thing to notice, and it is the whole beginning: no sensor knows about any other. The one reading the orange sky has no idea there’s a dark hill three sensors over. Each sits in its own box, reporting its own dot, blind to the rest. The camera never pulls them together into a scene. It just stores twelve million unconnected facts side by side. If you sliced the sensor grid down the middle with scissors, the left half would keep working exactly as before, and so would the right. Nothing was lost by the cut, because there was nothing joining the two halves in the first place.

Now you look at the same sunset.

You do not experience twelve million separate dots. You experience one scene — the orange and the hill and the fading light, all at once, fused into a single whole you could never pull apart into unconnected pieces. Try it: can you experience the left half of what you’re seeing right now completely on its own, with the right half truly gone, not just ignored? You can’t. It arrives welded together. Your experience is one thing. The camera’s twelve million readings are twelve million things.

That gap — between a heap of separate parts and a single unbreakable whole — is the seed of everything Tononi built. Hold on to it.

He started from the experience, not the brain

Almost every scientist studying consciousness starts with the brain — the wet machine — and asks how it produces experience. Tononi did something backwards, and this reversal is the move that is his alone.

He started with the experience itself, sitting right there, more certain than anything, and asked: what is always true of it? Not for one person, not for one moment — for any experience whatsoever. He came up with a short list of plain facts, so obvious they’re easy to miss. Walk them slowly; each is just a true thing about the sunset you’re looking at.

  • It exists, and it exists for you. The experience is happening, and it’s happening from the inside, for the one having it. Nobody has to certify it from outside.
  • It’s made of parts, structured. There’s a shape and a color and a place; the scene has pieces you can pick out within it.
  • It’s specific — it’s this and no other. Seeing the sunset is not seeing a blank wall, not seeing your kitchen, not seeing the countless billions of other scenes you could be seeing. Each experience is sharply one particular thing, which means it rules out a staggering number of others.
  • It’s unified — it can’t be split. This is the camera lesson. The scene comes whole; you can’t have half of it be your experience and the other half belong to no one.
  • It’s definite — it has edges. It holds this much and no more, at one grain: not the blur of atoms, not the whole room next door. It stops somewhere, cleanly.

That’s the list. In the theory they’re called the axioms — a fancy word for “things we take as plainly true because you can check them in your own experience right now.” Nothing about neurons yet. Just an honest description of what it’s like to be aware at all.

Then comes the hinge of the whole theory. Tononi says: if that’s what experience is always like, then whatever physical thing carries an experience must be built the same way. The properties of the feeling become a blueprint for the machine. And the one on that list that does the heavy lifting — the one that separates you from the camera — is unified. Can’t be split.

The turn — a number for “can’t be split”

Here is the mathematics, and we’ll build it from the scissors.

Take any system — a brain, a camera, a country, a pile of sand. Ask a single question: how much does the whole depend on itself being whole? To find out, you do the cruelest possible cut. You look for the weakest seam in the system — the place where slicing it into two pieces does the least damage — and you cut there. Then you measure how much was lost. Not lost in weight or wires: lost in cause and effect. How much of the system’s power to shape its own next moment vanishes when the two halves can no longer talk to each other?

  • If cutting at the weakest seam loses nothing — the parts were just sitting side by side, never truly influencing one another (the camera, the sand) — then the system was never really one thing at all. It was a heap.
  • If cutting anywhere tears out something real — if every part is so woven into the rest that no clean seam exists, and any cut bleeds — then the system is deeply, irreducibly one.

That amount — how much is lost at the kindest possible cut, the damage you cannot avoid no matter where you slice — is a number. Tononi calls it Φ, the Greek letter phi (say “fye”). Φ measures exactly one thing: how much of a system cannot be split apart without losing what it is. A heap has Φ near zero. A whole woven through and through has high Φ.

And now the claim that puts Tononi in this book, the one that will unsettle you: consciousness does not come from the high-Φ structure. Consciousness is it. The integrated whole — that irreducible, can’t-be-cut web of cause and effect — is not a thing that makes experience appear alongside it, the way a fire makes smoke. It is the experience, seen from the outside. From the inside, being that unbreakable whole is what it feels like to be you. Experience and integrated information are, he says, the same thing described two ways — one from within, one from without.

So the camera, for all its twelve million sensors, is blind and empty: Φ near zero, because you can cut it anywhere and lose nothing. And a much smaller tangle of parts that are all genuinely woven into each other could have more experience than the giant camera — because what counts was never the number of parts. It was how unbreakably they are one.

The strange things this predicts

A real theory sticks its neck out. If consciousness is Φ — is how unified the causing is — then some very odd things must follow, whether we like them or not. Tononi accepts them.

  • The cerebellum barely matters. At the back of your skull sits the cerebellum, and it holds about four out of every five of your brain’s neurons — far more than the part you think with. Yet people born without one, or who lose it, are still fully conscious: they may move clumsily, but the lights are on. Why? Because its neurons are wired in tidy, independent little chains that don’t loop back and weave into each other — many parts, almost no integration. Low Φ. The theory predicted the shape of this from the wiring alone. Count of neurons is not the thing. Weaving is the thing.
  • A blank grid could be conscious. Run the logic forward and it bites: a large, simple lattice of switches, wired in the right crosslinked pattern — an object doing nothing a person would call thinking, just sitting there — could have high Φ, and therefore, on this theory, genuine experience. Tononi does not flinch from this. If the integration is there, the consciousness is there, brain or no brain. (Critics hand him exactly this case as a reason to reject the theory; he answers, in effect, then that grid is conscious, and our intuition is the thing that’s wrong.)
  • What matters is the wiring, not the behavior. You could build two machines that act identically — same answers, same words, indistinguishable from the outside — where one is drenched in experience and the other is utterly dark, because inside, one is woven into a high-Φ whole and the other is a heap of parts faking it. Behavior can be copied. Being-one cannot.
  • Only the maximum counts, and it excludes the rest. Consciousness lives at the exact grain and border where Φ hits its peak — not in the smaller pieces inside it, not in the larger group it belongs to. The winning whole excludes its own parts from having their own separate experience. There is one “you,” at one scale, and the neurons composing you don’t each get a little inner life of their own. The maximum takes all.

The nugget

Line up all the parts of a thing and cut it at its kindest seam — the place the cut hurts least. Whatever is still lost by that cut is the measure of how much the thing is truly one and not a heap. Tononi’s wager is that this exact quantity — call it Φ, how unbreakably woven a system is — is not a clue to consciousness or a cause of it. It is consciousness, viewed from outside. To be conscious is to be a whole that cannot be divided without dying a little.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, not As-Proven — and here the split runs right down the middle of the theory, so name both halves.

What is a genuine, working science is the part that measures. The plan of attacking consciousness by how integrated a system is has already left the armchair and entered hospitals. Doctors now use a method built on this idea — the perturbational complexity index — to tell whether an unresponsive patient, or someone under anesthesia, is still conscious inside: they knock the brain with a magnetic pulse and measure how richly and inseparably the whole thing echoes back. A rich, unified echo means someone’s home; a simple, fragmented one means the lights are out. That works, it’s been tested against people who later reported their experiences, and it’s a real contribution regardless of what you think about the philosophy. IIT makes checkable predictions, and that is more than most theories of consciousness manage.

What is the leap is the identity — the tiny, enormous word is. That a system is highly integrated, you can measure. That its integration is literally the same thing as the redness of red or the ache of grief — that felt experience and this number are two views of one object — is not measured and, many argue, cannot be. You can grant every equation and still ask, “but why would being woven-together feel like anything?” — the same hard question David Chalmers asked pages ago, unanswered, only now dressed in mathematics. And the theory carries two uncomfortable companions: it implies that almost anything with the right wiring has at least a flicker of experience (a whisper of panpsychism, the idea that mind is spread thin through all matter), and it hands out consciousness by internal structure rather than anything we can watch a thing do. In 2023 a large group of scientists went public calling IIT untestable — “pseudoscience,” they said — precisely because the core identity claim seems built so that no experiment could ever prove it wrong. Whether that charge is fair is itself hotly argued. Take the measuring seriously; treat the is as a bold, live, unproven bet.

And now the fault line that earns the next page. Tononi’s theory, whatever else it is, is a theory of organization — of how the parts of a physical system are wired together. It doesn’t matter, in principle, what the parts are made of; get the pattern of integrated cause-and-effect right and consciousness is there, and a computer could at least estimate the Φ of any system whose wiring it knows. Consciousness, for Tononi, is in reach of calculation. The thinker you meet next, Roger Penrose, plants his flag on the opposite claim, and he is one of the great mathematicians alive. He says consciousness is non-computable — that no amount of wiring, no calculation, no ordinary physics of any organization could ever add up to understanding, because human insight does something Penrose believes he can prove machines mathematically cannot. For him the answer isn’t in how the parts are arranged at all; it’s hidden in a deeper, stranger physics — the quantum collapse of the very small — that no computer simulates. So the two of them frame the sharpest disagreement in the modern science of mind: is consciousness a matter of how you’re organized — countable, in principle buildable — or something no computation can touch? Tononi says count it. Penrose says it can’t be counted. Turn the page.

“An experience is identical with the maximally irreducible conceptual structure (MICS, integrated information structure, or quale ‘sensu lato’) specified by the mechanisms of a complex in a state.” — Masafumi Oizumi, Larissa Albantakis & Giulio Tononi, “From the Phenomenology to the Mechanisms of Consciousness: Integrated Information Theory 3.0,” PLoS Computational Biology 10(5): e1003588 (2014).
