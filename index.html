The Substrate Inquiry

The glass — one way of seeing what is

A living document, held As-If. Not a belief you have to hold. Not a theory anyone has to defend. It is a way of looking — told as a walk, not a lecture — offered so the mind has somewhere to stand while it thinks about what is.

Primary author: Leon Traverse. Written with Aria (Claude, Anthropic), 2026.

The fish

Imagine a fish born in a glass tank.

It has never known anything else. The tank is its whole world — the water, the light, the shapes that drift past the glass. And here is the thing the fish never thinks about, because it is too close to see: everything it has ever known about the room outside came to it through the glass. The blur of a passing hand, the warmth of the lamp, the dark when someone switches off the light — all of it arrived as a change on the surface of the glass. The fish has never once touched the room. It only ever touched the glass.

Most fish live and die without noticing this. But suppose one fish, one day, notices. It presses close and realizes the glass is not a small detail of its world. The glass is the world — or at least, it is the only edge where the world ever reaches in. Everything the fish will ever know has to cross that surface.

You are that fish. This is a document about the glass.

Your glass

You have never touched the world directly either. Everything you have ever known — every sight, every sound, every thought and memory — arrived as something happening inside your own experience. You have never once stepped outside yourself to check the world against it. Nobody has. It is not a flaw we can fix with a sharper eye or a better instrument. It is the shape of the situation. There is always a glass.

You already know how this works, because you use a machine built on the same trick. On a computer you drag files to the trash and open folders — but there are no tiny folders inside the machine, only voltages you will never see. The screen is an interface: a surface that shows you just enough to act, and hides the rest. You are not seeing the computer. You are seeing a helpful picture painted on glass.

The scientist Donald Hoffman argues your senses are exactly that kind of screen. Evolution did not build you to see reality as it truly is; it built you to see a simple, survivable picture. The apple, the color red, the solid table — useful icons on the glass, not the machinery behind it. What you call “the world” is the picture your looking paints. The world itself stays on the far side.

The one idea

Here is the whole document in a sentence: everything you can know about anything reaches you across a boundary — a glass — and the thing itself stays behind it.

You know a friend by their face and voice, the surface they turn toward you. You know a distant star only by the light that finally lands on you. You know your own body through your skin and senses. Always a boundary; always the interior sealed on the far side.

Now the part that makes this more than a nice thought. When you go looking, this same idea keeps turning up — discovered over and over by people who were not talking to each other and were not even working on the same question:

  • The physicists studying black holes found the holographic principle: everything knowable about a chunk of space is written on its outer surface, not spread through the inside. The inside turns out to be a kind of reconstruction from the edge.
  • The biologists describing what makes a living thing a thing use the Markov blanket: the boundary through which a cell, or a body, or a mind meets everything outside it.
  • Hoffman’s interface — your glass — is the same shape once more.

A physicist named Chris Fields looked at these and said: these are not merely similar. They are the same thing wearing different coats — the black-hole surface, the living membrane, the screen of your senses, even the plain outline you draw to call something a “thing.” One idea underneath all of them: a boundary that carries what can be known and hides the rest. That single idea is the keystone. Everything else in this document hangs from it.

Where the glass comes from

So what is an “observer”? Nothing spooky. An observer is just a place where the world gets split into an inside and an outside — a point of view. Draw a boundary anywhere and you have made an inside that looks out and an outside that gets looked at. The fish, the cell, the person, the star as seen from here — each is a glass with a world behind it.

And this comes with one hard fact you can check inside your own experience right now. You can never get around your own glass to see yourself from the outside. The eye cannot see itself seeing. Whatever you turn your attention to becomes a thing looked at — but the looker is always one step behind, doing the looking, never caught. Reach for it and you only find another object, with the real you still behind that. The fish can press against the glass forever and never get to the far side of its own looking. You are the edge of your world, not an item inside it.

This is old and it is solid. Kant said the subject can never become an object. Contemplatives spent centuries on it — “the seer is not the seen.” It even shows up as a proven mathematical limit: a system cannot fully contain a model of itself. Different people, different tools, same wall.

Three fish

Now the heart of it.

Three completely different kinds of seeker walked up to that glass from three different sides, none of them trying to agree with the others.

The physicist-fish chased what the world is made of at the very bottom — and kept finding that space and time are probably not the bottom. They seem to grow out of something more basic: information, and how it’s arranged. “Spacetime is doomed,” as one famous physicist likes to put it. And when they ask what remains at that level, they find the glass. One of them, Markus Müller, even proved — inside a small toy world — that if you start with nothing but an observer’s information and a simple rule for what it expects next, a stable, lawful, shared world builds itself, like a room assembling around a point of view that was there first.

The philosopher-fish never left the tank at all. Just by looking carefully at experience itself, they reached the same wall — the looker that can’t be looked at.

The biologist-fish asked a plainer question: what makes a self? Not a soul — just, where does one living thing stop and the world start? Their answer (Karl Friston, Michael Levin) is that a self is a boundary a living system keeps drawing around itself to stay alive. The glass again — now seen from the side of biology, by scientists assuming no magic whatsoever.

Three fish, three routes, one glass. That they arrived at the same place without coordinating is the single most interesting fact in this whole inquiry.

What you can see through it

Stand at the glass a moment, held lightly — As-If — and a couple of old puzzles soften.

Time. You feel time flow: past gone, future coming, a moving “now.” But physics has never found that flow out in the world; its deepest equations have no ticking clock at all. One clean reading: the flow is something that happens on your side of the glass, not out in the room. The world may simply be, all at once — the way a strip of film exists all at once, even though the movie only plays one frame at a time for whoever is watching. The “now” is where a point of view sits, not a moment the universe singles out.

Everyone’s glass. If reality is what shows up on boundaries, then no single view is the “real” one that cancels the rest. Your view and mine and the next fish’s are all real facets — different windows on the same room, none of them the master window.

What we are NOT saying (read this twice)

A map like this attracts people who want to run past its edges. So here are the fences, and they matter more than anything above.

  • We are not saying the universe is conscious, or that you are secretly God. The solid part is the structure — the glass, the point of view, the strange agreement between three kinds of seeker. Whether there is someone home on the far side of the deepest glass — whether reality has any inner life at the bottom — is the one question this map does not answer. It is an open door, not a conclusion. Tellingly, several of the serious people on these three roads reach the whole picture while flatly refusing to say anything about consciousness. That they get there without it is what keeps the rest honest.
  • We are not saying science is wrong. All of this sits on top of working physics; it’s a way of reading where physics already points, not a rebellion against it.
  • We are not offering answers you could never check. Where a claim could never be tested or felt, even in principle, we mark it as guesswork and leave it marked.

And the deepest honesty, the one that keeps the whole thing from tipping into arrogance:

Maybe we find “a glass everywhere we look” simply because a glass is how looking works — not because the world is made of glass. A fish might well conclude the universe is made of water. If every way of knowing anything runs through a point of view, then of course points of view will seem fundamental — we’d find that shape everywhere whether or not it’s the deep truth. And from inside our own glass, we cannot fully step out to check. So we hold even the central idea with open hands. The map may be describing the shape of seeing, not the shape of being — and we are honest that we can’t be sure which.

That humility is not a crack in the idea. It is the idea, done properly.

What it’s for

So why build it, if it proves nothing and claims little?

Because a map is not a belief. It doesn’t tell you what to hold as true; it gives you a place to stand and a way to move. This one takes a heap of the deepest, strangest questions — what is time, what is a self, what is real, what am I — and lays them out in one connected shape instead of a scatter of separate mysteries. It lets the mind hold them together and turn them over, without first having to settle anything unsettleable.

That is the whole offer: a structure for the mind to stand on. The fish doesn’t need to break the glass. It only needs to see the glass — and then, for the first time, it can begin to make out the shape of the bigger room, and past that, the bigger world.
