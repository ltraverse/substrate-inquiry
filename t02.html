The Scroll · Two

The Buddha

Cognizing is real — but there is no one who cognizes.

Twenty-five centuries ago, a man walked out of a palace to find out why living hurts. He is remembered as the Buddha — “the awake one” — and after long searching he sat down and looked, with enormous care, at his own experience: not at the stars or the gods, but at the plain traffic of seeing, hearing, feeling, thinking that is happening in you right now. On the page just before this one, the forest teachers of the Upanishads looked at the very same traffic and found, behind it all, one unchanging witness — the Self that is the ground of everything. The Buddha stood at that exact door, looked as hard as anyone ever has, and said: there is no one there. Only the looking. This page walks how a careful mind could possibly arrive at something so strange — one slow step at a time. Go gently; it is meant to be tried, not just read.

First, take yourself apart into what is actually here

Sit for a moment and ask a very simple question: what is my experience made of right now? Not in theory — right now. The Buddha’s answer was to sort the whole of it into five heaps. He called them the khandhas, which just means “heaps” or “bundles.” Watch them show up.

There is form — the body, the solid stuff, the sensation of the chair against you. There is feeling — not emotion yet, just the raw tone of each moment: pleasant, unpleasant, or neither (the cool of the air, the ache in a shoulder). There is perception — the quick act of recognizing, the mind going “chair,” “red,” “his voice,” sorting the flood into knowns. There are formations — the reactions and habits and wants that stir up in response: the leaning-toward, the pushing-away, the plans. And there is consciousness — viññāṇa — the bare fact of knowing something is there at all, the awareness in which the other four appear.

Now here is the first quiet surprise. Look for the you that owns these five. Look for the extra thing — the little self who is standing back and having all this. When you actually search, you find only more heaps: another feeling, another thought labelled “me.” You find the bundle. You never find the owner of the bundle. The Buddha’s claim is that the bundle is all there is — and that the sense of an owner is one of the things inside it, not something over it.

Look hard at the knower — because that is where we hide the self

Of the five, one feels different. Form, feeling, perception, reactions — fine, maybe those come and go. But surely the consciousness, the knower, the one that is aware of all the rest — surely that is me, the steady watcher behind my eyes?

A monk named Sāti thought exactly this. He decided that this same consciousness — one continuous knowing — was the real self, the thing that travels on from life to life, unchanged. When the Buddha heard of it, he called Sāti in and corrected him more sharply than almost anywhere in the texts (this is the Mahātaṇhāsaṅkhaya Sutta, MN 38), because Sāti had smuggled the old idea of a soul in through the back door and pinned it on consciousness.

Here is the correction, and it is beautiful. Consciousness is not one thing that stays. It is not an owner sitting behind the senses. It is discriminative knowing that flares up only at contact — when a sense meets its object. Eye meets a shape, and eye-knowing happens. Ear meets a sound, and ear-knowing happens. The knowing does not exist first, waiting; it arises because the meeting arises, and it is even named after the meeting. The Buddha’s image is a fire. A fire has no nature of its own that persists between fuels — you name it by what it burns. Wood makes a log-fire; grass makes a grass-fire; it is the same word “fire,” but there is no one flame that leaves the wood and moves into the grass. Blow out the fuel and there is no fire flying off somewhere to wait. So with knowing: eye-and-shape makes seeing; ear-and-sound makes hearing. There is no single master-consciousness underneath, touring from sense to sense, carrying “you.” Just knowings, each lit by its own contact, each gone when its contact goes.

That is why Sāti was wrong. Nothing transmigrates because nothing stays even from one blink to the next.

Test it the Buddha’s own way: is it yours to command?

He gives a second, even plainer test — a test you can run yourself. This is from the very first teaching he gave on this subject, the Anattā-lakkhaṇa Sutta (“the mark of not-self,” SN 22.59).

Ask of each heap: is this mine to rule? If the body were truly your self — your own — you could simply say “let it never fall ill,” and it would obey. You cannot. If feeling were your self, you could say “let only the pleasant arise”; it does not listen. If consciousness were your self, you could say “let my mind be exactly thus and stay there” — and hold one thought forever. Try it. It slips within seconds; a new knowing has already replaced the one you meant to keep.

So the reasoning is quiet and complete: each heap is impermanent (it arises and passes), and because it passes it brings dukkha, unease — you cannot lean your weight on a thing that keeps dissolving. And what is impermanent and not under your command cannot honestly be called “my self.” This is his word anattā — not-self: not “you have no experience,” but “nothing in experience is the fixed, owning self you assume.” The self was never one of the pieces. It was a reading laid over the pieces.

So what holds it together, if no one is holding it?

Now the fair objection. If there is no owner, no thread, why does experience hang together at all? Why does this moment seem to belong to the same “me” as a moment ago? If it is all loose flashes, what stops it flying apart?

The Buddha’s answer is not a thing but a law. Nothing here stands alone; each piece leans on the next. He put it in four small words: when this is, that is; from the arising of this, that arises. This is dependent origination — the plain rule that everything shows up because its conditions have shown up, like a shadow that appears exactly when the light and the object do, needing no separate shadow-substance. His picture for the two deepest partners — consciousness on one side, and name-and-form (the body-plus-naming, the rest of the bundle) on the other — is two sheaves of reeds leaned upright against each other. Neither stands on its own; each holds the other up; pull one away and both fall. So there is no first thing, no foundation-self underneath propping it all. There is a standing pattern of things mutually leaning — and that leaning is stable enough to look like a someone, without any someone being there.

Then where does the powerful feeling of “I” come from? He calls it asmi-māna — the conceit “I am.” It is a fabrication, a construction the process throws up about itself, the way a whirlpool in a river can seem like a fixed object though it is only water passing through a shape. The “I am” is real as a feeling that arises. It is false only as a report — it claims an owner that the looking never finds.

The chariot — how a thing can be real and empty at once

This last step rescues the whole idea from sounding like a denial of the obvious. A later teacher (in the Milindapañha, the “Questions of King Milinda”) used a cart to make it plain, and it is the cleanest handle of all.

Point at a chariot. Now: is the chariot the wheels? No. The axle? The pole? The frame? No single part is the chariot. Take every part away, one by one — is the chariot hiding somewhere among them, a chariot-substance over and above the parts? It is not. And yet — you are not lying when you say “chariot.” The word is true. “Chariot” is a dependent designation: a real and useful name for parts standing in the right relation, pointing at nothing extra behind them.

That is exactly the Buddha’s “you.” “Leon,” “me,” “the self” — a true, useful name for five heaps running in lawful relation, and no owner-substance behind the name. The word is not false. The thing it seems to promise — a separate someone who has the experience — is not there. Both at once. This is the razor’s edge his whole view balances on: the knowing is real and lawful; a knower is not.

The nugget

There is seeing, but no separate seer; there is knowing, but no knower standing behind it. What you call “I” is a true name for a bundle of happenings leaning on each other by law — not the owner of them, but one more thing the bundle keeps producing. The experience is real. The one you assume is having it is a story the experience tells about itself.

And this was not cold cleverness. He taught it as a release: if there is no fixed self, there is no fixed self to defend, to lose, to be wounded — and the grip that drives our suffering, the endless clutching for “me and mine,” has nothing left to hold.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, not As-Proven — and notice, right away, that it opens onto a real fight, not a tidy agreement.

The Buddha and the Upanishad teachers began at the identical discovery, the one you can check in yourself in sixty seconds: you cannot catch the seer. Turn to look at the one who is looking, and you find only more things looked-at. Both traditions saw this clearly and neither flinched. That much is solid, first-person, testable now.

Then they walked opposite ways out of the same door — and this is the point, not a mess to clean up. The Upanishads said: you cannot catch the witness because you are it — it is the one awareness underneath everything, the ground of all being, ever-present, never an object. The Buddha said: you cannot catch the witness because there is none — pull the layers back and you do not reach a silent watcher, you reach the end of watchers; there is only watching, arising and passing by conditions. One turned the un-catchable into the deepest Something. The other turned it into a genuine No-one.

Which is right cannot be settled from outside — that is the leap, on both sides. What each claims about what is there (a universal Self / no self at all) goes beyond what the sixty-second experiment can show; the experiment shows only that the seer is not findable, and stays silent on whether that means it is everything or nothing. Two of the most careful minds who ever lived stood at one door and could not agree — and every thinker still to come in this book is, in some way, trying to stand in that doorway and see straight. The disagreement is not a bug in the tradition. It is the exact shape of the hard problem, drawn twice.

“Consciousness is reckoned by the very condition dependent upon which it arises. When it arises dependent on the eye and forms, it is reckoned as eye-consciousness… Just as a fire is reckoned by the particular condition dependent on which it burns — a log-fire, a grass-fire — so too consciousness is reckoned by its condition.” — Majjhima Nikāya 38, Mahātaṇhāsaṅkhaya Sutta (rendering of the Pali)
