The Screen · Nine

Keith Frankish

Consciousness is a trick the brain plays on itself.

Every page you have read so far treats one thing as solid ground: that your experience really does have a felt inner glow — a what-it-is-like — and the job is to explain where that glow comes from. This page belongs to a living philosopher who stops and says, gently but seriously: wait. What if the glow isn’t there? Keith Frankish defends a view called illusionism, and it is the one genuine dissenter in this whole book. It is easy to hear it as a joke or an insult — “you’re saying I don’t really feel anything?” — and easy to wave it away. Don’t. It is a careful, honest position held by careful, honest people, and the only way to see why is to walk it slowly, from something you can check with your own eyes right now. Take it step by step; the sting is in the tail.

Start with a line you can’t stop seeing wrong

Look at two lines. One has little arrowheads at each end pointing out, like this: >—<. The other has arrowheads pointing in, like this: <—>. The second line looks longer. Clearly longer.

Now take a ruler and measure them. They are exactly the same length.

Here is the strange part, and the part that matters. Measure them, prove it to yourself, know with total certainty that they are equal — and then look again. The one still looks longer. Knowing the truth does not fix your seeing. Your eyes go on reporting something you have proven false. You cannot switch it off.

This is a famous trick called an optical illusion, and everyone has felt one. But notice exactly what it shows: your own inner report about your own experience can be flatly wrong, and stay wrong even after you catch it. The thing seeming a certain way is not the same as the thing being that way. Hold onto that, because Frankish is going to aim it somewhere you have never aimed it before.

Your brain lies to you about how much you’re seeing

Try one more thing, right where you sit.

Look straight at one word on this page and hold your eyes still. Now, without moving them, try to read the words three lines down. You can’t. Away from the exact center, your vision is a smeary mess — poor detail, washed-out color. Almost the entire visual world you feel you are having, right now, is actually a blur. Only a tiny dot in the middle is sharp.

So why does it feel like you are bathed in a wide, rich, crystal-clear picture of the room? Because your brain tells you it is so. It doesn’t fill in the missing detail — it does something sneakier: it doesn’t flag that anything is missing. It hands you a confident report of “rich, complete, detailed scene” when what it actually has is a sharp dot and a lot of guessing. And you believe the report, because the report is all you ever get to check against.

There’s a cousin of this that scientists have tested carefully, called change blindness: people stare right at a scene while something big changes — a wall shifts color, a person is swapped for someone else — and they simply don’t notice, even while feeling sure they’re seeing everything. Again and again, the felt fullness of experience turns out to be more than is really there. The inner report oversells.

Now we have two facts, both checkable, both a little humbling: your inner sense of your own experience can be wrong, and it routinely is wrong — it paints a richer, cleaner, fuller picture than the machinery actually supports. Frankish’s whole move is to ask one more question with these facts in hand.

The turn — what if the “felt glow” is the biggest oversell of all?

First, the thing everyone in this book has been trying to explain. Philosophers have a word for the felt inner quality of experience: qualia (KWAH-lee-uh). The pure redness of red. The stab of a pain, over and above the flinch and the “ow.” The taste of coffee as it is for you, on the inside. Not what your eyes do, not what your mouth reports — the felt extra, the what-it-is-like. Almost every thinker here treats that felt extra as the one rock-solid fact, the thing that couldn’t possibly be an illusion. Descartes’ descendants all agree on this much: you might be wrong about the whole outside world, but you can’t be wrong that it feels like something to be you.

Frankish points at that rock and says: that is exactly where the illusion lives.

Here is his claim, laid out plainly. Your brain does a great deal of information processing — sorting light into “red,” steering your hand off a hot stove, running the machinery of sight and taste and pain. And your brain also builds a simplified inner picture of that very processing — a quick cartoon of what it’s doing, so you (the whole animal) can use it, report it, act on it. That inner cartoon is crude and distorted, the way the rich-visual-field feeling was crude and distorted. And the specific distortion it introduces is this: it represents plain physical processing as if it had these special, glowing, ineffable felt qualities — as if there were a mysterious extra, the qualia. There isn’t. There is just the processing, and a built-in report that dresses it up as something more. The “felt glow” is the brain’s oversell of its own workings, the same trick as the too-rich visual field, turned all the way up.

So — and this is the sentence that makes illusionism illusionism — it is not that you have no experiences. You see, you hurt, you taste; those states are perfectly real and they matter. What Frankish denies is that they carry the special phenomenal properties philosophers say they do. You are not wrong that you’re in pain. You are wrong about what pain is like — you think it has a raw felt essence over and above its workings, and that “over and above” is the illusion.

Feel how this flips the whole board. For everyone else, the great mystery — the hard problem — is: why does all this processing feel like anything at all? Frankish says that question has a false word buried in it. It assumes there’s a real felt glow to be explained. Take the glow to be an illusion, and the hard problem doesn’t get solved — it dissolves. There was never an extra thing there to explain.

But a new problem takes its place, and Frankish takes it dead seriously. If there’s no glow, then we have to explain why it seems so utterly, unshakably like there is one. Why is this particular illusion the most powerful, most convincing illusion a mind can have — so convincing that the smartest people alive stake everything on it being real? He calls this the illusion problem, and he says this, not the hard problem, is the real work of a science of consciousness: not explain the felt magic, but explain the trick — how the brain generates so overwhelming a sense of a magic that isn’t there.

The nugget

You cannot be wrong that you’re having an experience — but you can be wrong about what the experience is like. Illusionism says the “felt inner glow,” the special what-it-is-like that every other view treats as bedrock, is the brain’s cartoon of its own plain workings, oversold the way your eyes oversell the rich detail that isn’t really in the room. So the hard problem — why does processing feel like something? — isn’t answered; it’s un-asked, because it smuggled in a glow that was never there. The real puzzle is why the trick is so good.

Hold it honestly

Hold this As-If, like every page — but this one needs a different kind of honesty, because this is the view the rest of the book is arguing against, and the fair thing is to give it real weight before you say where it hurts.

What is genuinely strong about it. Illusionism is the most deflationary view here — it asks for the least. Every other page pays for the felt glow with something expensive: new physics, or consciousness as the fundamental stuff of reality, or a special extra ingredient the universe has to supply. Illusionism pays nothing extra. It says the physical brain, doing ordinary physical work, is the whole story, and the only thing left to explain is a very good trick — which is the kind of thing brains are already known to do (the arrowed lines; the too-rich visual field; change blindness are all receipts). It takes the science exactly as it is and adds no mystery. That is not a cheap position; it is arguably the most scientifically modest one on offer, and it is held by serious philosophers, not cranks. It is a live, respectable answer — not a dodge, and not a denial that your inner life is real. Read carelessly it sounds like “you’re a robot who feels nothing.” Read carefully it says: you feel everything you think you feel; you’re just wrong about the metaphysical price tag on it.

What it pays for that modesty. A steep, strange intuitive cost — maybe the steepest bill in the book. There is one thing that has always felt like the single fact you cannot be wrong about: that right now, it feels like something to be you. Doubt the outside world, doubt your memories, doubt your own body — fine — but the sheer thereness of present experience seems to be the one place where seeming and being can’t come apart, because here the seeming is the being. Illusionism has to say: even there, you can be fooled. And many thinkers find that not just hard to believe but almost incoherent — an illusion, they point out, is itself an experience; to have the illusion of a glow already is to have some glow. Whether Frankish can wriggle out of that is exactly where the fight is. So the honest cut is this: illusionism is the strongest, cleanest deflation anyone has managed — and it asks you to disbelieve the one thing that feels least deniable. Take both halves seriously; the price is real and so is the position.

Now the disagreement — and it is the hinge of the whole book. Remember David Chalmers, back in the Ten, the one who named the hard problem and made it the floor beneath this entire field — the fixed point everyone since has had to build on. Frankish’s claim is aimed straight at that floor. He says the floor itself is the illusion. There is no polite middle here: either the felt glow is real and the hard problem is the deepest question there is, or the glow is a trick and the hard problem is a category mistake dressed as a mystery. Chalmers built the room; Frankish says the foundation is painted on.

And it isn’t only Chalmers. Look back across the Living pages and see how alone Frankish stands: Hoffman takes consciousness as the fundamental fabric behind the physical world; Kastrup makes it the one universal mind; Goff finds a spark of it in every particle; Tononi builds a whole science to measure how much of it a system has. Every one of them treats the felt inner reality as the bedrock fact to be honored and explained. Frankish treats that same bedrock as the very thing to be explained away. He is the one voice in the choir singing the opposite note — and a book that only sang the felt-experience-is-real note would be lying to you about how open this question really is. That is why he is here, at full strength.

Which leaves us needing something rare: not another grand claim, in either direction, but a genuinely honest floor — someone who will lay out, without flinching, exactly how little any of us are actually entitled to say. Turn the page to Chris Fields.

“Illusionism is the view that phenomenal consciousness (in the philosophers’ sense) is an illusion.” [verbatim — opening line of the abstract; widely reproduced across secondary sources, primary paywalled at the time of writing] The illusionist’s move is to replace the hard problem — why physical processing feels like anything — with what Frankish calls the illusion problem: the task of explaining why we are so powerfully and systematically disposed to judge that our experiences have felt phenomenal qualities, when they do not. [paraphrase, confirmed across multiple sources] — Keith Frankish, “Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness,” Journal of Consciousness Studies 23(11–12), 2016.
